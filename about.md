# Sources & Methodology

## How This Knowledge Base Was Created

This knowledge base represents an **original interpretive work** that:

1. **Synthesizes** concepts from academic literature on epistemic virtues
2. **Interprets** theoretical frameworks for practical application
3. **Creates** original tutorials, guides, and explanations
4. **Applies** research-based concepts to educational contexts

## Relationship to Source Material

**What this is:**
- An independent synthesis and interpretation of published ideas
- Original applications of academic concepts to practical scenarios
- Educational commentary on virtue epistemology research

**What this is NOT:**
- A reproduction or paraphrase of copyrighted books
- A substitute for reading the original academic works
- A comprehensive summary of any single author's complete argument

## Primary Academic Sources

### Jason Baehr
- *The Inquiring Mind: On Intellectual Virtues and Virtue Epistemology* (2011)
- *Cultivating Good Minds: A Philosophical & Practical Guide to Educating for Intellectual Virtues* (2015)

**How used:** Baehr's framework of intellectual character virtues provides the conceptual foundation for virtue definitions and relationships explored in this KB.

### Joëlle Proust
- *The Philosophy of Metacognition: Mental Agency and Self-Awareness* (2013)

**How used:** Proust's work on metacognition informs the connection between epistemic self-evaluation and virtue development.

### Matthew Lipman
- *Thinking in Education* (2003)
- Various works on Philosophy for Children

**How used:** Lipman's community of inquiry approach informs pedagogical strategies in tutorials and how-to guides.

## Citation Practices

Throughout this knowledge base:
- **Ideas** are attributed to their originators with inline citations
- **Direct quotes** are minimal, properly cited, and used only for key definitions
- **Applications and examples** are original creations for this project
- **Further reading** sections direct users to original sources

## Fair Use Considerations

This work qualifies as fair use under U.S. copyright law (17 U.S.C. § 107) as it is:
- **Transformative**: Creates new practical applications and educational materials
- **Educational**: Serves non-commercial educational purposes
- **Limited**: Uses only concepts and ideas, not substantial portions of original text
- **Non-substitutional**: Complements rather than replaces original works

Users are encouraged to consult original academic sources for complete theoretical treatments.
