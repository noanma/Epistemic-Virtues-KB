    Intellectual Virtues Overview

    The intellectual virtues described here are drawn primarily from Jason Baehr’s framework and reflect those he considers most essential for good inquiry and education. This list is intentionally incomplete and meant as a starting point for further exploration, not a definitive inventory.

    Source: Baehr, J. (2011). The Inquiring Mind. Oxford University Press

    Attentiveness

    Definition

    The habit of being "personally present" in the learning process, helping individuals avoid distractions and dispersion and focus on what is essential. This virtue involves being mentally engaged, noticing important details, and giving appropriate focus to the task at hand.

    Key characteristics:

    • Maintains focus and concentration
    • Notices important details
    • Remains mentally engaged in learning

    Autonomy

    Definition

    The ability to think and form beliefs for oneself. This virtue involves independent thinking while remaining appropriately responsive to evidence, expert guidance, and reasonable criticism. An intellectually autonomous person doesn't simply accept what others say but thinks critically and forms their own reasoned judgments.

    Key characteristics:

    • Thinks independently and critically
    • Forms beliefs based on personal reasoning
    • Remains appropriately responsive to evidence and expertise

    Carefulness

    Definition

    The willingness to detect and avoid methodological errors and mistakes, resulting in a search for accurate conclusions and results. This virtue involves being thorough in one's thinking, checking work for errors, and striving for accuracy and precision.

    Key characteristics:

    • Checks work for errors and mistakes
    • Strives for accuracy and precision
    • Avoids hasty or careless reasoning

    Courage

    Definition

    The disposition that enables one to sustain one's ideas in the face of opposition or difficulty. This virtue involves being willing to ask difficult questions, challenge assumptions, speak up when necessary, and persist in inquiry even when it's uncomfortable or socially risky.

    Key characteristics:

    • Willing to ask difficult questions
    • Stands up for well-reasoned beliefs
    • Persists despite social pressure or discomfort

    Curiosity

    Definition

    The disposition to ask thoughtful and insightful questions, driven by a desire to understand and learn. Curiosity initiates the learning process by motivating inquiry and exploration. An intellectually curious person actively seeks knowledge, asks meaningful questions, and pursues understanding beyond surface-level information.

    Key characteristics:

    • Asks thoughtful and insightful questions
    • Actively seeks new knowledge and understanding
    • Shows genuine interest in learning

    Fair-mindedness

    Definition

    The disposition to evaluate claims, evidence, and arguments impartially, without bias toward one's own views or interests. This virtue involves treating all perspectives and arguments with equal consideration based on their merits.

    Key characteristics:

    • Evaluates arguments impartially
    • Avoids favoritism toward one's own views
    • Gives equal consideration to competing claims

    Generosity

    Definition

    The disposition to interpret others' views and arguments charitably, giving them the benefit of the doubt and considering them in their strongest form. This virtue promotes productive dialogue and mutual understanding.

    Key characteristics:

    • Interprets others' views charitably
    • Considers arguments in their strongest form
    • Shows goodwill toward other thinkers

    Humility

    Definition

    Intellectual humility is the disposition to recognize the limits of one's knowledge and be appropriately aware of one's intellectual fallibility (Baehr, 2011).

    Being aware of one's intellectual limitations and being willing to acknowledge, own, or take responsibility for them. This virtue involves recognizing the limits of one's knowledge, being open to the possibility of being wrong, and acknowledging mistakes when they occur.

    Key characteristics:

    • Recognizes the limits of one's own knowledge
    • Willing to admit mistakes and errors
    • Open to learning from others

    Inquisitiveness

    Definition

    A sustained desire to know, understand, or learn. Similar to curiosity but emphasizing a persistent questioning attitude and ongoing engagement with intellectual matters.

    Key characteristics:

    • Maintains persistent questioning attitude
    • Shows sustained interest in understanding
    • Actively pursues knowledge over time

    Open-mindedness

    Definition

    The capacity to listen to points of view that are opposed to one's own in an understanding way, being able to take up an alternative point of view and give it a fair and honest hearing. This virtue helps overcome the obstacle of cognitive bias and enables consideration of perspectives that differ from one's own.

    Key characteristics:

    • Considers alternative viewpoints fairly
    • Listens to opposing perspectives
    • Avoids bias and prejudgment

    Rigor

    Definition

    The disposition to be thorough, precise, and exacting in one's thinking and inquiry. This virtue involves maintaining high standards for evidence, reasoning, and intellectual work.

    Key characteristics:

    • Maintains high intellectual standards
    • Demands precision and thoroughness
    • Applies exacting methods of inquiry

    Tenacity (Perseverance)

    Definition

    The disposition to persist in intellectual tasks despite obstacles, setbacks, or difficulties. This virtue involves sustained effort in the face of intellectual challenges and not giving up when learning becomes difficult.

    Key characteristics:

    • Persists through intellectual challenges
    • Maintains effort despite setbacks
    • Doesn't give up when learning is difficult

    Thoroughness

    Definition

    Investigating topics in depth and exploring the connections among them from a comprehensive perspective. This virtue involves not settling for superficial understanding but probing deeply into subjects and exploring how ideas relate to one another.

    Key characteristics:

    • Investigates topics in depth
    • Explores connections between ideas
    • Pursues comprehensive understanding
